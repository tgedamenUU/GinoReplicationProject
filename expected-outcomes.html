Anticipated positive and negative outcomes of promotion into leadership, coordinated by Tom Damen (Utrecht University).

Overview

This block covers Studies 5–7, which investigate how men and women differ in their expectations about the positive and negative consequences of being promoted into a leadership role. The central idea is that women may anticipate more costs (e.g., conflict, stress, work–life strain), which can reduce their motivation to pursue leadership.

Original studies (Gino et al.)

Study 5 – Working adults imagining a promotion

In Study 5, working adults imagined being promoted into a leadership role. They then rated how likely various positive outcomes (e.g., growth, recognition, impact) and negative outcomes (e.g., stress, conflict, work–family interference) would be if they accepted the promotion.

Main finding. Women anticipated more negative outcomes than men, even when positive expectations were similar. These more negative expectations were associated with lower motivation to pursue leadership roles.

Study 6 – Executive sample

Study 6 replicated the imagined-promotion scenario with senior executives. They evaluated similar sets of positive and negative outcomes of accepting a leadership promotion.

Main finding. The gender pattern from Study 5 replicated among high-level professionals: women expected more negative consequences of leadership roles than men, suggesting that these expectations are not limited to early-career or student samples.

Study 7 – Undergraduate students

Study 7 adapted the scenario for undergraduate students, who imagined being promoted into a campus leadership role. Students rated anticipated positive and negative outcomes, and reported their interest in pursuing such leadership positions.

Main finding. Negative expectations were a strong predictor of lower leadership interest among women. In mediation analyses, negative outcomes partially accounted for gender differences in leadership motivation.

Replication protocol

Replications in this block follow a shared protocol. Student teams adapt the scenario to their context (e.g., workplace, student organisation) while preserving the core structure of positive and negative outcome expectations.

Required variables

  • Positive outcome ratings for the imagined promotion into a leadership role.
  • Negative outcome ratings for the same scenario.
  • Gender and basic demographics (e.g., age, occupational / study status).
  • Optional but recommended: leadership interest or willingness to accept the promotion.

Required analyses

  • Test gender differences in negative outcome expectations.
  • Test gender differences in positive outcome expectations.
  • If leadership interest is measured, examine whether negative outcomes predict leadership interest and whether this relationship differs by gender.

Exploratory add-ons

Exploratory extensions might include measures of burnout expectations, work–life balance concerns, leadership self-efficacy, or social identity. These variables can be used to test additional mediators or moderators of the link between gender and leadership motivation. All such work must be clearly labelled as exploratory and documented on OSF.
