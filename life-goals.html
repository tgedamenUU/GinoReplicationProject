Overview

This block covers Study 1 and Study 2 of Gino et al. (2015). Both studies examined whether leadership emerges as part of people’s long-term life goals, and whether there are systematic gender differences in the presence of leadership-related goals.

Original studies (Gino et al.)

Study 1 – Life goals in working adults

In Study 1, working adults recruited online were asked to freely list their most important long-term life goals in an open-ended format. Independent coders then classified whether any of these goals reflected leadership aspirations, such as becoming a manager, leading a team, or holding a position of influence at work.

Main finding. Men were significantly more likely than women to spontaneously include leadership-related goals. This suggested that gender differences in leadership motivation already appear at the level of broad personal ambitions and self-generated life priorities.

Study 2 – Conceptual replication with refined coding

Study 2 used a similar online sample and the same open-ended life goal task, but with a refined coding scheme to improve reliability. Participants again listed their life goals, and coders again evaluated whether leadership aspirations were present.

Main finding. The gender difference observed in Study 1 replicated: women were less likely than men to mention leadership as part of their life goals. This reinforced the idea that early, self-defined life goals may contribute to gender gaps in leadership motivation.

Replication protocol

Replications in this block follow a shared protocol to ensure comparability across cohorts. Pre-registration is not required; instead, teams follow the protocol and document any deviations in their OSF project and in the replication quality checklist.

Required variables

  • Open-ended life goal responses (in the original language of data collection).
  • Coding of whether each participant listed at least one leadership-related goal, using the shared coding scheme.
  • Participant gender, age, education, and employment status.

Required analyses

  • Test for gender differences in the likelihood of listing leadership-related goals (e.g., logistic regression or χ² test).
  • Report effect sizes (e.g., odds ratios, Cohen’s d based on proportions, or regression coefficients), and confidence intervals whenever possible.

Exploratory add-ons

Student teams may add extra variables and exploratory analyses, such as self-determination theory measures (autonomy, competence, relatedness), leadership self-efficacy, or social identity scales. All additional variables and analyses must be clearly labelled as exploratory and documented on OSF.
