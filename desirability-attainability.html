Overview

This block covers Study 3 and Study 4, which focus on how men and women evaluate leadership roles in terms of desirability and attainability.

Original studies

Study 3 – MBA alumni

MBA alumni rated how desirable and attainable they perceived high-level leadership roles to be. Desirability captured how attractive they found such roles; attainability assessed whether they believed people like them could realistically obtain such positions. Main finding: Men and women found leadership roles similarly desirable, but women rated them as significantly less attainable. This suggested that perceived barriers, rather than interest, may explain gender gaps.

Study 4 – Online sample

An independent online sample completed the same desirability and attainability ratings. Main finding: The key pattern replicated: • Attainability gap: Women again perceived leadership as less attainable. • Desirability: Little to no gender difference. This reinforced the idea that perceived access—not interest—is central to gender differences in leadership motivation.

Replication protocol

Required variables

  • Desirability rating(s) for leadership roles
  • Attainability rating(s) for leadership roles
  • Gender, age, education, employment / student status

Required analyses

  • Gender differences in attainability
  • Gender differences in desirability
  • Effect sizes and, optionally, models where attainability predicts leadership interest

Exploratory add-ons

Student teams may include additional measures (e.g., leadership self-efficacy, perceived barriers, stereotype endorsement) and exploratory analyses, documented on OSF.
